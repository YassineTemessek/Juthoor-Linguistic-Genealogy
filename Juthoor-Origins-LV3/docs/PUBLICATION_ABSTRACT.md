# Publication Abstract: The Arabic Consonantal Root System as a Computational Key to Cross-Linguistic Genealogy

## Title
**Juthoor: Computational Evidence for Systematic Cross-Linguistic Consonant Correspondences Anchored in the Arabic Root System**

## Authors
Yassine Temessek, with computational assistance from Claude (Anthropic) and Codex (OpenAI)

## Abstract

We present Juthoor ("Roots"), a computational linguistics engine that investigates cross-linguistic relationships through systematic analysis of the Arabic consonantal root system. Drawing on a corpus of 2.64 million lexemes across 11 languages, our system discovers and validates consonant-level correspondences between Arabic and Indo-European, Semitic, and other language families.

Our approach is distinguished by three methodological innovations: (1) a root-centric discovery engine using 12 independent etymology linking methods, evaluated against 1,889 gold benchmark pairs across 13 language pairs; (2) empirical validation through a consonant correspondence matrix derived from 3,461 alignments, revealing that the Arabic pharyngeal consonant ع (ʿayn) is deleted in 88% of Indo-European correspondences — the strongest single cross-family sound correspondence in our dataset; and (3) a convergent evidence framework identifying 153 Arabic roots with independent matches in three or more target languages.

Internal analysis of the Arabic root system (LV1 Research Factory) establishes four empirically supported properties: binary root nuclei define coherent semantic fields (>11σ above baseline), consonant order is semantically meaningful (p=0.014), positional semantics follows a hierarchy where position 1 carries the most weight (86% of letters show significant positional effects), and root meaning is 71.6% predictable from consonant structure alone.

These findings are organized into 10 documented "corridor cards" — systematic sound correspondence pathways — of which 8 are statistically confirmed through automated cross-language replication testing. A cross-language cognate graph comprising 12,455 nodes and 47,071 edges provides the evidentiary foundation for theory synthesis.

While the observed patterns are statistically significant, we emphasize that directionality of influence, the distinction between inherited cognates and loanwords, and chronological validation remain open challenges requiring expert linguistic review.
